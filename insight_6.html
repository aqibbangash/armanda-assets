The fallacy of dreams

Published on September, 2019

“Sometimes dreams do come true”, said every motivator, influencer, actor or keynote speaker each time they wanted to leave you with a sense of wonder. All our lives we have been taught to follow our dreams and sacrifice for them. Never were we told on the act of following itself. How do you do it? We toil to achieve our personal benchmarks of success and wealth. We daydream about our dream house, dream car and dream everything. But are we doing it right? Will we achieve our dreams if we keep on dreaming?

Dreams without plans are just wishes and, unless you find a dusty old lamp in the desert, wishful thinking will not lead to career or material success. What we mean is, working hard in the same job all your life will not give you financial freedom. Financial freedom requires thorough planning, learning from past oversights and self-education. We define the fallacy of dreaming as trying to achieve success by stationary, unplanned and miscalculated desire.

Fortunately, information is a touch away and if planning is not for you there are investment companies and financial planners that will assist you to achieve independence.

Let's bestow some economic wisdom while we are at it with some market commentary:

  • Equities may continue to struggle over the rest of the year, as investors adjust to the prospect of a long trade war and more weakness in the global economy.
  • It’s important to put these moves in perspective, particularly those in risky asset markets, as the S&P 500 is currently still within 5% of its all-time high.
  • Jerome Powell’s much-anticipated speech hinted that the US Fed will cut interest rates at its September meeting, but did little to clarify its intentions beyond that

Considering retirement doesn't start in your later years. Being aware of the lifestyle you'd like to maintain during your retirement can, and should, influence financial decisions you make now. Simply, planning for retirement means planning for the best financial future possible, now. According to the ABS, we've been spending around 30 years in the workforce, retiring at around 55.3 years. However, over the recent 5-10 years, that timeframe has increased, bringing us to 62.9 years for the average age of retirement.

That's a long time to be working. For some of us, our goal to retire early heavily influences our money decisions, especially as owners with existing mortgages are retiring despite their debt. If this sounds like you, and your focus is on getting ready for retirement, what are other people actively doing to prepare?

You could consider diversifying your savings to build passive income. Superannuation continues to be the most common form of investment, as it’s required by law for employers to contribute to. However, you could decide to put funds into other avenues and earn interest through a term deposit or passive investment. Savings accounts and term deposits are considered safe, but often only offer a maximum return of 2.73% over a 5 year period.
