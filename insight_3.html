The Minimum Wage Work Ethic?

Published on August 14, 2019

It is said that you can’t dream of being a millionaire with a minimum wage work ethic. The first time I heard this was in one of those online video ads you have to see before the actual video begins. Typically, I brush my mind off this motivational and easy money-making non-sense as soon as the ad stops playing, but somehow the term minimum wage work ethic stuck with me. I had some questions about the whole concept; is the minimum wage work ethic habitual for employees making minimum wage? Is it an attitude towards your job? Is it people who do the bare minimum? Or is it open to interpretation? What exactly is the minimum wage work ethic?

According to the 2019 household income and labour dynamics in Australia report, the typical Australian household is actually worse off now than during the global financial crisis in 2008. Statistically, real median household annual disposable income was $80,595 in 2017, $542 lower than it was in 2009 at $80,637, having fallen between 2009 and 2011, risen in 2012 and remained unchanged after that. Even though the real wages have increased over this period.

Our spending patterns have changed drastically over the last ten years, it’s a global spending epidemic. The desire to spend more than we earn which is fuelled by the buy now pay later campaign, has made a sizeable effect on the disposable cash we had 10 years ago. This spending pattern has taken flight from the desire to become millionaires, without the effective work and informed decision making that comes with it. It is more important to be perceived as a millionaire than to be one in reality.

Recent statistics show that people spend more on their credit cards than their debit cards. Narrowing it down to two averages, the average credit card purchase is $114.69 while the average debit card purchase is $46.82. Australians spend more than they can afford. This is synonymous with other global economies. This is also fuelled by the points campaign with rewards and offers that only 70% of the card holders claim back. With the credit card annual fee and an average of 12% p.a. interest this gap of $67.67 between debit and credit card average purchases is just the tip of the iceberg. I call this gap the “Greed Gap”. The greed gap grows with a baseless compulsion to purchase items that give a fleeting high of achievement.

Stuck in a dead end job, with bills to pay and bored to death, we will hunt for an opportunity to get that feeling of achievement, that new car smell, that satisfaction when a new TV is turned on or when you set-up a new cell phone with all the bells and whistles that come with it. We start investing in the greed gap rather than ourselves and our future. Making it larger, making it take over our collective behaviour as reasonable and informed consumers. No matter how minimum our wage is, we will try to work the hours required just so we can achieve the bare minimum credit rating that enables us to acquire the items that will give us a short-lived but legal euphoria. As per my findings, this is the minimum wage work ethic. Spend more, save less and jump from one high to the other.

Needless to say, eliminating liabilities and accumulation of assets is the answer. I try to use the multi-layered pyramid in my decision making.

The pyramid is a simple allocation of the types of investments that I would use to secure my future earnings. This may not be rocket science, but it is easy to grasp and applicable to novice investors. The rule is to start from the bottom and then include each layer in your portfolio going forward. It is only fair that beginners start with less risky options and riskier investors with more experience receive more profits as they move up the pyramid. Each stage of the pyramid represents a specific time period with a minimum period of 12 months. It is a good measure to use while making investment decisions, but it is no way, financial advice.

Lastly, we must learn to stand up and walk before we can fly, and a strong footing is always the first step. With secured investments like term deposits and bonds offering a fraction above the inflation rate in Australia, it is better to look for alternatives. Fixed income products and guaranteed leases aim to offer a fixed flow of income which reduces the greed gap. Fixed income products give money to you, increasing your cash flow and savings with minimum risk.
